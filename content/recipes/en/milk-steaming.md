# Milk Steaming Techniques: Latte vs. Cappuccino

The difference between a latte and a cappuccino is not just the ratio — it is the texture of the milk. Mastering the steam wand on the **Casadio Nettuno** is the single skill that separates average milk drinks from exceptional ones. This guide breaks down the exact wand technique, temperature targets, and textural goals for each drink.

---

## Core Concept: The Two Types of Milk Foam

| Property | Microfoam (Latte) | Thick Foam (Cappuccino) |
|---|---|---|
| Bubble size | Microscopic — invisible to the eye | Visible fine bubbles (2–4 mm) |
| Texture | Velvety, paint-like, pourable | Thick, dense, spoonable |
| Volume increase | ~20–30% above original milk volume | ~50–100% above original milk volume |
| Mouthfeel | Silky, integrated with coffee | Airy, distinct layer on top |
| Appearance | Glossy surface — ideal for latte art | Matt-white cloud sitting above espresso |

---

## Ideal Temperature Targets

- **Target zone (both drinks):** **60 – 65 °C** (140 – 150 °F)
- **Maximum:** Do not exceed **70 °C** — milk proteins denature, producing a flat, scalded taste and destroying foam structure.
- **Minimum:** Do not serve below **55 °C** — the drink feels lukewarm and foam collapses quickly.

> **Pro Tip:** If you don't have a thermometer, use your hand as a rough guide — the jug should be **too hot to hold comfortably** but not painful (approximately 65 °C). Always use a thermometer for calibration training.

---

## Equipment Setup

- Use a **stainless steel steaming jug**:
  - Small (350 ml) for single drinks
  - Medium (600 ml) for two drinks or larger portions
- Fill to just **below the spout notch** (~1/3 full) — milk doubles in volume as you steam.
- **Purge the Casadio Nettuno steam wand for 1–2 seconds** before use to expel condensed water.
- Wipe the wand with a damp cloth **immediately** after every use.

---

## Latte Milk Technique (Microfoam)

### Goal
Silky, velvety microfoam with a glossy surface — no visible bubbles. The texture should pour smoothly and support latte art.

### Wand Position
- Submerge the steam wand tip **1–2 cm below the milk surface**.
- Angle the jug (~30°) so the wand tip points off-centre to create a spinning vortex.

### Aeration Phase (Stretching) — ~3–5 seconds
- At the start, position the tip **just at the surface** (half in, half out) to introduce a controlled amount of air.
- You should hear a **gentle hissing sound** — not loud ripping. Loud ripping = tip too far out = large bubbles.
- Aerate for **3–5 seconds only** — lattes need very little additional air.

### Texturing Phase (Heating) — remainder of steaming
- Submerge the wand tip fully (~2 cm below surface).
- Milk should spin in a fast, even **whirlpool vortex** — this integrates and breaks down bubbles into microfoam.
- Continue until the jug reaches **60 – 65 °C**.

### Finishing
- Tap the jug firmly on the counter **2–3 times** to collapse any remaining large bubbles.
- Swirl in a circular motion for 10–15 seconds to keep foam integrated with liquid milk.
- Pour immediately — microfoam separates quickly.

## Cappuccino Milk Technique (Thick Foam)

### Goal
A thick, dense foam layer that sits proudly on top of the espresso — dense enough to support a sprinkle of cinnamon or cocoa without sinking.

### Wand Position
- Position the tip **at the milk surface** (barely submerged — 0.5–1 cm below).
- Tilt the jug slightly to create a gentle spin.

### Aeration Phase (Stretching) — ~8–12 seconds
- Keep the wand tip very close to the surface for a longer stretch — **8–12 seconds** of aeration.
- You should hear a slightly more pronounced hissing/frothing sound than with a latte.
- Milk volume should visibly increase — watch the jug fill up significantly.

### Texturing Phase (Heating)
- Once you've achieved sufficient volume (~50% increase), submerge the tip slightly more (~1.5 cm) to heat and refine the foam.
- Priority now: **heating, not adding more air**.
- Stop at **60 – 65 °C**.

### Finishing
- Do **not** tap the jug — for cappuccino, you want to retain the airy structure.
- Gently swirl to keep homogeneous.
- Use a spoon to **hold back the foam** while pouring the hot milk into the espresso first, then spoon the thick foam on top.

## Side-by-Side Quick Reference

| Step | Latte | Cappuccino |
|---|---|---|
| Jug fill level | ~1/3 full | ~1/3 full |
| Wand tip depth | 1–2 cm | 0.5–1 cm |
| Aeration time | 3–5 seconds | 8–12 seconds |
| Target temp | 60–65 °C | 60–65 °C |
| Final texture | Pourable microfoam | Thick, spoonable foam |
| Foam-to-liquid ratio (approx.) | 1:4 foam to milk | 1:1 foam to milk |
| Latte art possible? | Yes | Limited (dry cap) |

---

## Common Mistakes & Fixes

| Mistake | Effect | Fix |
|---|---|---|
| Wand too far out of milk | Large, soapy bubbles that collapse | Submerge tip deeper |
| Over-aerating a latte | Thick, frothy texture — pours like cappuccino | Shorten aeration to < 5 s |
| Under-aerating a cappuccino | Flat, watery foam that sinks | Extend aeration to 10+ seconds |
| Steaming past 70 °C | Scalded milk taste, flat foam, no latte art | Use thermometer; stop at 65 °C |
| Not purging Casadio wand | Water dilutes milk at the start | Always purge 1–2 s before steaming |
| Not wiping wand | Milk bakes onto wand, harbours bacteria | Wipe immediately after every use |

---

> **Texture Test:** After texturing, run your finger across the surface of the jug. Latte milk should feel completely smooth — no bubbles. Cappuccino milk should feel like thick, cool whipped cream on top.
