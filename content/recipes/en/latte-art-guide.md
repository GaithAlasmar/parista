# Latte Art Guide

Latte art is the final 10% that transforms a technically correct drink into a memorable experience. Master the fundamentals, then build the patterns. Every pattern starts with the same foundation: **perfect microfoam**.

---

## Prerequisites — Non-Negotiable

Before attempting any pattern, confirm:

✅ **Milk temperature: 60–65°C**
✅ **Microfoam texture: silky, glossy, "wet paint" surface — zero visible bubbles**
✅ **Fresh espresso with a visible crema layer**
✅ **Pitcher: pointed-spout (latte art pitcher) — 360ml or 600ml recommended**
✅ **Pour within 10 seconds of steaming** (foam integrates within the milk after steaming — pour while it's still perfectly combined)

---

## The Milk Pouring Foundation

All latte art uses the same two-phase pour:

1. **High pour (blending phase):** Hold the pitcher 8–12cm above the cup. Pour at a steady rate to blend the milk with the espresso and build a canvas.
2. **Low pour (art phase):** When the cup is ~70% full, lower the pitcher to the surface (~1–2cm). This is where you create the pattern. The microfoam now sits on top instead of sinking.

---

## Pattern 1: Heart ❤️

**Difficulty:** Beginner | **Best for:** First latte art lesson

### Steps:
1. Begin the **high pour** to blend — pour steadily until the cup is ~70% full.
2. Lower the pitcher to the surface (~1–2cm from the milk).
3. **Pour a white circle** by moving the pitcher slightly back and forth (or in a small circle). The white foam should appear as a circular bloom.
4. When the circle reaches your target size, **lift the pitcher slightly and drag forward** in a single, swift motion — cutting through the white circle to create the V-point of the heart.
5. The drag should be fast and decisive — hesitation creates a smeared line.

### Common Mistakes:
- Dragging too slowly → smeared line, not a V
- Cup not tilted enough → foam sinks, no surface bloom
- Too much milk in the cup before lowering → no room for the heart

## Pattern 2: Tulip 🌷

**Difficulty:** Beginner–Intermediate | **Best for:** Cappuccinos and lattes

### Steps:
1. High pour to blend until cup is ~60% full.
2. Lower the pitcher to the surface. **Pour a small white dot** (pulse the wrist briefly to release a blob of foam), then STOP.
3. Move back slightly. **Pour a second dot** behind the first (it will push the first dot forward slightly). STOP.
4. Move back again. **Pour a third dot** (even larger) behind the second. STOP.
5. **Drag forward** in one swift motion through all three dots — the connecting line creates the "stem" of the tulip.

### Notes:
- Each dot should be a deliberate pulse — pour-stop, pour-stop, pour-stop.
- The dots naturally stack into a petal formation as you progress.

---

## Pattern 3: Rosetta 🍃

**Difficulty:** Advanced | **Best for:** Showcase drinks, full lattes

### Steps:
1. High pour to blend until cup is ~65% full.
2. Lower the pitcher to the surface, close to the far edge of the cup.
3. **Wiggle the pitcher rapidly left-right** with small, even movements while moving the pitcher back toward you at a slow, steady pace. This creates the frond/leaf pattern.
4. As you reach the near edge of the cup, **straighten the wiggle and draw a clean line** forward through the pattern to create the centre stem.
5. The final stroke should be fast and single — pull straight through the middle.

### Notes:
- The wiggle speed must be consistent — too slow = wide, spread fronds. Too fast = narrow, indistinct fronds.
- Practice the wiggle motion before attempting on a real drink — use a water/milk mixture first.

---

## Pattern 4: Swan 🦢

**Difficulty:** Expert | **Best for:** Competition and VIP presentation**

The Swan combines multiple patterns. Build in order:

1. **Body (Rosetta):** Create a Rosetta on one side of the cup — this forms the swan's body/wing.
2. **Wing addition (Tulip):** Add a small tulip or additional frond layers to build out the wing mass.
3. **Neck:** Pour a long, sweeping S-curve from the body toward the centre-front of the cup.
4. **Head and beak:** Add a small round dot at the end of the neck. Drag a tiny "beak" outward.

> The Swan should only be attempted after mastering all other patterns individually. It is a synthesis of all techniques.

## Rules for Successful Latte Art

| Rule | Detail |
|---|---|
| ✅ Cold milk starting temperature | **4°C** — always start with fridge-cold milk |
| ✅ Microfoam quality | Silky, zero visible bubbles, glossy surface |
| ✅ Milk temperature target | **60–65°C** — never higher |
| ✅ Fresh espresso | Pull the shot immediately before pouring — don't let it sit |
| ✅ Pour timing | Pour within **10 seconds** of finishing steaming |
| ✅ Use a latte art pitcher | Pointed spout gives control — wide spout loses definition |
| ❌ Boiling milk | Destroys foam structure, kills art |
| ❌ Grainy foam | Can't create patterns with large bubbles |
| ❌ Hesitation during patterns | Commit to each movement — confidence = clean art |
